5.

Evolution and rationality: Evolution tends to favor systems that act rationally because rational behavior generally increases survival and reproductive success. The primary "goal" shaped by evolution is the propagation of genes. Systems that make better decisions in pursuit of survival and reproduction are more likely to pass on their genes, leading to the emergence of increasingly sophisticated and rational cognitive systems over time.

6.

AI as science or engineering: AI is both a science and an engineering discipline. As a science, it involves the study of intelligence and the development of theories about how intelligent behavior can be created. As an engineering discipline, it focuses on building intelligent systems and applying AI techniques to solve real-world problems. The field involves a continuous interplay between scientific inquiry and practical application.

7.

Computer intelligence: The statement "computers can do only what their programmers tell them" is an oversimplification. While computers follow instructions, modern AI systems, especially those using machine learning, can develop behaviors and solutions that weren't explicitly programmed. They can learn from data, generalize to new situations, and sometimes surprise their creators with novel approaches. Therefore, this statement doesn't necessarily imply that computers cannot be intelligent.

8.

Animal intelligence: The statement "animals can do only what their genes tell them" is also an oversimplification. While genes provide a blueprint for neural development and some instinctive behaviors, many animals, especially higher mammals, demonstrate learning, problem-solving, and even cultural transmission of knowledge. Their behavior is a complex interplay of genetic predispositions and environmental influences. This statement doesn't preclude animal intelligence, as intelligence can emerge from the interaction between genetic potential and environmental learning.
